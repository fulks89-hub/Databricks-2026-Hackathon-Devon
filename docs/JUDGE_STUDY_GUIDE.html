Asclepius — judging study guide

Read from this during the 3-minute demo + Q&A. Every point maps to where it shows in the app. Honesty is our edge — lead with it.

The pitch (say this first — ~20s)

Asclepius turns ~10,000 messy, AI-scraped Indian health-facility records into decisions a non-technical NGO planner can actually trust. One app spans three jobs — can a facility do what it claims? (Trust Desk), where are the real care gaps? (Medical Desert), and what must be fixed before we trust the data? (Readiness Desk) — and it is relentlessly honest about which numbers are verified versus self-reported.
OUR EDGE — OPEN AND CLOSE WITH THIS

Honesty is the product

Anyone can build a directory. We built the thing that tells a planner what NOT to trust — and twice we chose the harder, honest path over a better-looking number.

Honesty story #1 — we deleted our own headline stat Our first corroboration pass flagged 83% of claims “unsupported.” Instead of shipping a scary number, we proved it was an artifact: marketing blurbs don’t list commodity gear (no hospital writes “we own an ECG machine”). So we made corroboration positive-only — “no evidence in the text” is shown as neutral, never “can’t do it.”
Honesty story #2 — we declined the VPN The strongest specialty-level source (PM-JAY) is geo-fenced to India. We proved it was the portal, not our network — then declined a VPN bypass on ethics and used peer-reviewed PLoS research + official S3WaaS government data + the NABH registry instead. Honest provenance over a sketchy grab.

1Product judgment

Clear user? Thoughtful workflow + tradeoffs?

  • One real user, named: a non-technical NGO planner / coordinator deciding where to send people, money, and clinicians. Not a data scientist — no SQL, no jargon.
  • The workflow mirrors their job, not the data model: pick a persona on the landing page, then find care → find the gaps → fix the data. Each is a screen, each ends in an action (save a priority, patch a record, refer a patient).
  • The hard tradeoff, made on purpose: we under-claim. We’d rather say “self-reported” than falsely say “verified.” A planner who over-trusts a bad record sends a patient to a clinic that can’t help — so the product is tuned for precision over coverage.
  • Closed the loop: a planner doesn’t just read — they review, override, merge, and flag, and those edits reshape what every other view shows.
“We built this for one person — an NGO planner with no technical background — and every screen ends in a decision they can act on, not a dashboard they have to interpret.”

Where it shows

  • Landing / persona chooser (Patient · Clinician · Hospital · Medical Planner).
  • /planner deserts → /readiness fix-the-data → /facility/:id verify-the-claim — the planner’s three moves.
  • /readiness one-click suggested fix per row + click-to-call / mailto — non-technical remediation.

2Evidence & uncertainty

Outputs grounded in citations? Uncertainty handled honestly?

  • Every claim is graded, not asserted. For each facility × claimed service we show a trust tier, and we quote the verbatim source text it rests on:
Authority-verifiedCorroboratedSelf-reportedConflicting evidence
  • Stratified and labelled: ~16% authority-verified (an independent NABH certificate behind it) vs ~84% self-reported — and every score says which tier it rests on. We never average the two into a fake single number.
  • Uncertainty is a first-class output, not a disclaimer. Per-record data-confidence, sparse-field flags, and the signature data-poor vs real-gap split tell a planner which scarcity scores to trust.
  • The AI assistant can’t hallucinate a fact: the server re-anchors every quote as a literal substring of the real record; if nothing anchors, it asks a clarifying question instead of guessing.
  • Positive-only corroboration (honesty story #1): “no evidence in the text” = neutral, never “can’t do it.”
“Nothing here is asserted — it’s cited. Each capability carries a trust tier and the exact source text. And we’re loud about uncertainty: only one in six claims is authority-verified, and we tell you which.”

Where it shows

  • /facility/:id the four claim tiers, the “✓ NABH-certified” badge linking the official scope PDF, “How we read this record” (free-text → parsed fields with confidence chips), and the quoted evidence block.
  • /planner the per-service Evidence panel (nearest claiming facility + verbatim claim) and the “unknown supply — data-poor” badge.
  • AI assistant inline [facility · field] citation chips + a confidence band (no number) or a clarifying question.

3Technical execution

Works live? Databricks used well?

  • It’s live right now — one Databricks App on Free Edition, login-gated, serving all three desks.
  • A real medallion pipeline: Unity Catalog gold → Lakebase synced tables → this app. Heavy analytics live in UC; the app reads low-latency Lakebase Postgres; planner edits persist to a writable Lakebase OLTP schema.
  • Five Databricks tools, one app: Lakebase (reads + writes), Foundation Models (the grounded assistant, Llama-3.3-70B), Vector Search, the SQL warehouse, and the provided FDR + NFHS datasets.
  • Well-architected for honesty & change: the data layers swap behind stable views, and the trust tiers, desert scores, and readiness queue are all computed in the lakehouse — not faked in the front end.
“This isn’t a mockup. It’s Unity Catalog gold tables synced into Lakebase, read by a live Databricks App, with Foundation Models powering the assistant — five Databricks tools working together.”

Where it shows

  • The whole demo is the proof — it’s loading live from Lakebase as you click.
  • AI assistant a Foundation-Model answer, grounded server-side.
  • /readiness a planner edit writes back to Lakebase and the queue updates.

4Ambition

Went beyond the minimum, meaningfully?

  • One app spans three tracks — Trust Desk (T1), Medical Desert (T2), Data Readiness Desk (T4) — as a single coherent planner workflow, not three demos.
  • An independent authority tier (NABH): we went and pulled the official accreditation registry and parsed each facility’s certificate/scope PDF for audited, specialty-level scope + sanctioned bed counts. That’s evidence the provided dataset doesn’t contain.
  • The signature insight — data-poor vs real-gap: a per-district data-confidence layer so a planner knows a “0-facility” district is a missing-data artifact, not proven absence. That distinction is the difference between a useful tool and a misleading one.
  • Access, not counts: the desert score is per-capita severity = need × proximity (care-tier distance bands) + a population-burden overlay — rolled specialty → capability → area.
  • A grounded, agentic assistant that ranks the data on demand and asks for context instead of refusing.
“The minimum was answer one track. We answered three in one workflow, added an independent accreditation tier the dataset never had, and built the one thing a directory never gives you — a map of where the data itself can’t be trusted.”

Where it shows

  • /atlas the national coverage × condition map (and the bivariate “act-here” overlay).
  • /facility/:id the NABH authority badge + cert PDF.
  • /planner + /readiness the data-poor-vs-real-gap split, surfaced on both.

Anticipated judge questions — honest answers

Short, honest, no hedging. Lead with the honest part.

Why is only ~16% “verified”?

Because only ~16% have an independent authority — an official NABH certificate — behind them. We refuse to call the other 84% “verified” off a facility’s own marketing. The 16% is a floor of audited truth, not a ceiling of our coverage, and every score says which tier it rests on.

Isn’t “no evidence” just missing data?

Exactly — which is why corroboration is positive-only. Our first cut flagged 83% “unsupported”; we proved that was an artifact (blurbs don’t list commodity gear like ECG), so “no evidence in the text” is shown as neutral, never “can’t do it.”

How is this different from a directory / Google Maps?

A directory lists claims; we grade them. Every capability carries a trust tier + cited source text, the desert map weights by access not counts, and the Readiness Desk lets a planner fix the data and see what’s a real gap versus a data artifact.

What in here is NOT trustworthy?

The ~84% self-reported claims (shown as self-reported), the data-poor districts (0 mapped facilities = a join gap, not proven absence), and sparse fields like capacity (~25% filled). We surface all of it — never hide it. That honesty is the feature.

Why not scrape facility websites or use PM-JAY for richer data?

PM-JAY is geo-fenced to India (we proved it’s the portal, not our network) and we declined a VPN bypass on ethics. ~35% of scraped homepages were dead or wrong-entity — circular evidence. We used peer-reviewed PLoS + official S3WaaS government data + NABH instead.

Does it actually work live, and is it really Databricks?

Yes — it’s loading live as we click: Unity Catalog gold → Lakebase synced tables → this app, with Foundation Models powering the assistant. Five Databricks tools in one app.

Can a non-technical NGO worker really use this?

It’s built for exactly that person: choose a persona, work a queue with one-click suggested fixes and click-to-call, save priorities — no SQL, no jargon. The complexity lives in the lakehouse, not in their way.
